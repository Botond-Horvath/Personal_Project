Exoplanets, of which this project revolves around, are planets that can be found outside of the solar system, or in other words are planets that revolve around stars (whatever types they may be) other than our own Sun. As we know, and we have known for centuries, our own planet, and many others such as Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune revolve around the Sun as planets. However, after we discovered that stars in the sky are just like our Sun, or in other words our Sun is a star itself, we came to wonder if there could be planets revolving around some of these stars too. However, we did not have the means to detect them. Regardless, astronomers like Kepler and mathematicians like Newton gave us a new understanding of this vast universe, centered around strict physical laws, that as well as to the Solar System, applied well beyond to the vastess of the cosmos. Once telescopes advanced, we were able to observe so called binary stars, where two stars revolved around each other under the effect of gravity. Once instruments became even more perculiar, we began to observe smaller and smaller companions in binaries, until we were actually able to observe planets!

The first planet outside our solar system was discovered in 1995, using a method known as radial velocity this method had previously been used to detect binary stars. The planet was deemed 51 Pegasi b, thus initiating the nomenclature for exoplanets (the name of the parent star followed by b/c/d for planets) Another popular method used is known as the transit method, and looks at periodic dips in the brightness of stars to detect a possible blocking planet. This method, again, evolved from a method that was previously used to detect binaries.
