The World of Stars

Stars, unlike exoplanets, have been known and observed ever since humans existed. When there is a clear night, anyone could just go outside and observe these often awe inspiring testaments to our universe. Stars form therefore the cradle of all that we know of astronomy today. So what are stars, and what can we know about them?

Well, stars in short are large spheres of matter that are so massive that they are able to produce their own heat, through nuclear reactions that take place inside their cores, and radiate out from them in the form of electromagnetic radiation, which could be visible, infrared in the form of heat, UV, or many other forms of light. All stars are made up mostly of Hydrogen, like most other objects in the universe, and this is what stars fuse in the main portion of their lives in their core. However, they also contain Helium, and then trace amounts of Oxygen, Nitrogen, and other metals usually in ionized states. We know this from observing the spectrum of these stars, explained in the observations section.

The image above is an image of the star 51 Pegasi found in the constellation Pegasus. It is barely visible by the human eye. In constellations, the names of the stars are preceded by greek letters if the star is part of the constellation. If a star is insignificant in that it is found only in the territory of the constellation but not as a part of it, it is usually given a number. Therefore, 51 Pegasi is barely visible and does not form part of the Pegasus constellation, but is found in its territory.

The name of this star might already be familiar. The reason is that the first exoplanet ever discovered, 51 Pegasi b (exoplanets are given lowercase letters after the star name, starting with b) was indeed discovered as orbiting this star. So naturally, since it is necessary to know parameters of the parent star before calculating the parameters of the exoplanet orbiting it, we are going to demonstrate the calculations on this star, so we will have them for the exoplanet calculations too. The below sections run through the different parameters of the stars, and how they are calculated.

Summary

Through the last few sections we have come to understand on a much deeper level the parents of exoplanets: stars, and how astronomers are able to measure, calculate, and derive what they know about stars. Knowing about stars is crucial to know about exoplanets, and in fact any time that an exoplanet is discovered orbiting a star, whether through the radial velocity, transit, or any other method, the astronomer first needs to get familiar with the parent star before he/she can dive into calculating any parameter for the exoplanet. Now, calculating exoplanet parameters will be a walk in the park, since mostly the same laws will be applied as were applied to stars.

Before continuing to calculate the parameters of 51 Pegasi b, the first exoplanet discovered, let us summarize what we have measured and calculated in this section for its parent, 51 Pegasi:
